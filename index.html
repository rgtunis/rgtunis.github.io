Hi, I'm
Rachel Tunis

I defended my PhD from the University of Texas at Austin in April 2026. My work blends qualitative, quantitative, and design methods to explore how wearables and health tracking tools can effectively support self-care and improve outcomes for people living with chronic conditions, with a particular focus on psychosocial factors. I'm passionate about tailored design strategies that build capacity for users to manage their health and sustain engagement over time.

Background

Experience

Withings Summer 2023 & 2024

Design Research Intern

I joined Withings as part of the Design & Product Management team based in their Paris office for internships in both 2023 and 2024. During my first internship, my focus was on the app experience for users receiving alerts about heart rhythm irregularities — contributing to the design of several heart health features and creating an educational program for users with atrial fibrillation. During my second internship, I focused on the user experience for individuals with hypertension, conducting in-depth research including survey analysis of nearly 4,000 users, developing personas, and presenting UX strategy recommendations to the team.

UT Austin School of Nursing 2022 – Present

Clinical Research Associate & Graduate Research Assistant · Digital Heart Health Lab

Throughout my PhD, I worked on the clinical research team of an NIH-funded randomized controlled trial assessing how digital health technologies impact self-management behaviors among adults with heart failure. I worked directly with participants, led data analysis using quantitative and qualitative methods, and authored several peer-reviewed publications from this work. I am currently managing undergraduate and graduate students on offshoots of this project, including exploring applications for hypertension and the potential of an AI chatbot to support heart failure patients in their self-care.

FasterCures · Milken Institute 2018 – 2021

Senior Associate · Health & Technology

I worked at FasterCures, a Center of the Milken Institute for three years exploring issues at the intersection of health data and biomedical research — including bias in health datasets, regulatory gaps in health data protections, and the interoperability of health data systems.

Doctoral Work

Dissertation

My dissertation examined the mundane, day-to-day interactions people have with health technologies, and how those interactions shape psychosocial factors critical to long-term self-care — such as self-efficacy, motivation, and sense of autonomy.

Study 1

A secondary analysis of longitudinal RCT data exploring relationships between individual-level psychosocial factors, self-care behaviors, and engagement with digital health technologies among adults with heart failure.

Study 2

A 10-day diary study with individuals with Type 2 diabetes who use health tracking technologies such as CGMs — employing user-centered design methods to address unmet needs in day-to-day self-care.

This work contributed to the design of self-care technologies that support users in building confidence, resilience, and a genuine sense of agency over their health.
